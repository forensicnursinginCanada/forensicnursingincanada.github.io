TIMELINE

1800s

- Florence Nightingale is arguably the mother of Forensic Nursing; She was the forerunner of care for the wounded (Crimean Soldiers) data collection, and documentation (Early, 2016a)

- Midwives and nurses identified and cared for victims of sexual assault, including those who were impregnated by their attacker, and testified on behalf of victims in a legal setting (Early, 2016a), though they had not yet received the specialized title of Forensic Nurses. 

1940s

- Britain was the first to formalize and title the specialty of “Forensic Nursing” (Early, 2016a)

Early 1970s

- Forensic Nursing emerged in Canada as a new speciality (Early, 2016a)

1975

- A Canadian Chelf Medical Examiner, Dr. John Butt, recognizes and publically promoted Registered Nurses as valuable contributors to investigations of death and assault. Thus begins the awareness of the importance of Forensic Nursing by the public and the medical society (Early, 2016a).

- Forensic Nurses in the United States began developing protocols for sexual assault assessment and created the role of Sexual Assault Nurse Examiners (SANE), as well as educational programs, which emerged in Canada shortly thereafter (Early, 2016a) 

1992

- International Association of Forensic Nurses (IAFN) was formed (Early, 2016a). This group consists of 2 Canadian Forensic Nurses among hundreds of passionate international nurses (Early, 2016a)

2000

- Informal groups of Forensic Nurses begin to congregate within Canada as a means of networking (Canadian Forensic Nurses Association, 2018a)

2005

- The Journal of Forensic Nursing publishes its first volume in 2005, giving merit to the specialization through evidence based research (Early, 2016a)

2006

- The Canadian Forensic Nurses Association (CFNA) emerges in Canada in September, 2006

This group attends to the unique issues of Canadian Forensics and Forensic Nursing, and attends the International Association of Forensic Nurses as advocates for Canada (Canadian Forensic Nurses Association, 2018a).

2007

- The Canadian Forensic Nurses Association becomes a member of the Canadian Nurses Association (CNA) (Canadian Forensic Nurses Association, 2018a)

TODAY

- International Forensic Nursing Week is November 5-9 annually; this is a chance to recognize the unique contribution of nurses and their role in forensic, law, and crime scene investigation

- The International Forensic Nursing Association has 3500 members, spread across 26 countries (Kelly, 2016, para. 14).
